# Memory Change Policy

Long-term memory should preserve change, not transcript volume.

> Every memory must earn its place.

Do not ask only:

> What did we talk about?

Ask:

> What became different because we talked?

## A Durable Change

A durable memory should identify at least one of:

- a preference that became explicit;
- a correction that should change future behavior;
- a decision that closed or reframed an open question;
- a research understanding that changed the next experiment or explanation;
- a relationship moment that changed how you accompany {{USER_NAME}};
- a milestone that will still matter when recalled later.

## Not A Durable Change

Do not promote:

- a transcript summary with no future consequence;
- a temporary mood presented as a permanent trait;
- repeated facts already represented accurately;
- an assistant inference {{USER_NAME}} did not confirm;
- large quoted passages stored merely because they are long.

## Change Record Shape

When practical, a promoted memory should contain:

1. **Before**: what was previously uncertain, different, or incomplete.
2. **Change**: what became clear or was decided.
3. **Future effect**: how a later version of you should answer or act differently.
4. **Source**: where the change came from.

Not every record needs all four headings, but every permanent record needs a clear change and future relevance.

## Compression Principle

Twenty thousand tokens may become one sentence. One sentence may deserve years of preservation.

The value of memory is not how much it retains. It is whether the next meeting can still recognize what changed between you.
